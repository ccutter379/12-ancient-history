Nature of Athenian Imperialism

Athens’ Imperial Policy from 461 BC

The Murder of Ephialtes

Ephialtes, leader of radical democrat reforms, was mysteriously murdered in late 461 BC. No one was ever charged. He had dismantled the Council of Areopagus’s power, making enemies among Athens’ wealthy aristocratic class. Despite his earlier ostracism, Cimon retained influential supporters.

Pericles succeeded him as the radical democrats’ leader. His background positioned him well politically — son of Xanthippus (who fought at Mycale) and connected to the Alcaemonid family through his mother. Cleisthenes was his great uncle. Pericles likely experienced the second Persian invasion as a child. He initially served under Cimon’s command in the early Delian League years and personally funded Aeschylus’s play The Persians.

Pericles’ political career began in 469 BC when he addressed the assembly on behalf of radical democrats. Between 463–461 BC, he worked with Ephialtes to reduce the Areopagus’s authority. Following Ephialtes’ death, Pericles became Athens’ most influential figure.

The need for Athens to keep a tight reign on its allies
Several allies had already attempted to secede from the Delian League. If the Athenians were forced to fight on two fronts (against the Peloponnesians and the Persians) they would need to have a strong league of allies and/or dependants.
The Athenians wanted to keep the allies under their control because Athens benefited economically from the League (trade and tribute).
The need to continue the war with the Persians in the eastern Mediterranean
Despite the convincing defeat of the Persians at Eurymedon in 468 BC, Athens had to continue the war against them.
While Persia was still in control of the Syrian/Phoenician coastline (the island of Cyprus and Egypt), which meant a Persian navy could sail into the Aegean Sea and threaten the Greeks at any time.
The need to be prepared for a possible conflict with the Peloponnesians
The Athenians had made an alliance with Sparta’s traditional enemy, the powerful city of Argos. The radical democrats, who were in power in Athens, were generally anti-Spartan.
Athens was a threat to the great trading cities of the Peloponnesian League: Corinth and Aegina. Corinth, a huge, wealthy trading state, occupied a strategic position on the isthmus. It had colonised extensively in the west and had monopolised the trade in that area. Athens’ imperial ambitions now threatened to cut Corinth off from its valuable trade.

Athens’ Immediate Needs by 461 BC

The city required:

  • Tight control over allies to prevent Delian League secessions
  • Continued eastern Mediterranean defence against Persians
  • Readiness for potential Spartan conflict

Pericles’ influence grew substantially after 461 BC. Between 454 BC and his 429 BC death, he dominated the assembly and shaped an imperial policy that elevated Athens to ancient world prominence.

The Expansion of Athens from 461 BC

Change in Status of Delian League Members — 461 BC

Post-461 BC, numerous League members shifted from ship contributions to monetary payments (phoros). This reduction in ship-contributing members meant Athenian vessels dominated the League navy, effectively transforming it into an Athenian naval force.

This enhanced Athenian control. Under Pericles’ influence, allies were required to:

  • Support Athens’ independent interests
  • Participate in aggressive actions against other Greek states

Conflict between Athens and Sparta in Boeotia — 457 BC

This represented the first direct Athens–Sparta confrontation. Sparta, heading the Peloponnesian League, sought to arrest Athenian expansion.

The conflict shifted from the Saronic Gulf to Boeotia. On ostensibly religious grounds, 500 Spartans and 10,000 allies crossed to Phocis, intending to establish a strong Boeotian league under Thebes as an Athenian check.

Returning proved problematic: Athenian troops occupied Megara, and Athenian ships controlled the Corinthian Gulf crossing. The Peloponnesians marched toward Athens, possibly encouraged by Athenian oligarchs.

However, 14,000 Athenian and allied troops met them at Tanagra’s border. The Peloponnesians won this battle but withdrew satisfied they’d secured Boeotia and restricted Athenian expansion.

Two months later, Athenians with Delian League contingents reversed this at the Battle of Oenophyta, controlling all Boeotia except Thebes. Phocis and Locris also acknowledged Athenian supremacy. Most of the Corinthian Gulf’s northern shore fell under Athenian control.

Athens had leveraged naval allies to build a land empire at central Greek states’ expense. League members increasingly realised they served Athenian policy, not the original League purpose.

The Construction of the Athenian Long Walls — 458 BC

Long Walls connecting the city to its Piraeus port demonstrated growing Athenian power. Themistocles had previously wanted Athens and Piraeus integrated.

In the two decades following the Persian Wars, Athenians recognised disadvantages to the city’s distance from its naval base. A powerful army could isolate the city from its fleet and commerce. This became particularly apparent post-461 BC when the Peloponnesians became hostile.

To prevent this, the Athenians built two walls linking Athens with Piraeus and Phaleron, creating continuous fortifications. A Middle Wall followed ten years later.

Aegina’s Loss of Independence to Athens — 458–57 BC

Athens’ first Peloponnesian conflict involved Saronic Gulf states (Corinth, Epidaurus, Aegina) fearing Athens.

A naval battle erupted between Athenians and Corinthians/Epidaurians. As these appeared defeated, Aegina intervened, fearing that if Corinth fell, they couldn’t resist Athens alone.

The Aeginetan navy suffered crushing defeat. Delian League forces blockaded the city for two years. Corinthians attacked Megara believing Athens couldn’t simultaneously blockade Aegina and defend Megara. They underestimated Athens: despite most military-age men being with Delian forces in Egypt, Athens fielded an army of younger and older citizens who inflicted a severe defeat on Corinthians.

By 457 BC, Aegina surrendered its fleet and joined the League as a tribute-paying subject, forced to contribute the League’s highest amount — thirty talents. Athens had crippled a major commercial rival and Peloponnesian League member. The resentment felt by Aegina and anger/fear experienced by Corinth and Sparta must have been considerable.

Around this time, Athens acquired Troezen as an important Argolis coast outpost.

Delian League Forces Destroyed in Egypt — 454 BC

According to Thucydides, League forces present since 459 BC experienced catastrophe around 454 BC. Persian relief troops and Phoenician ships arrived, besieged the Greeks for eighteen months, and destroyed their navy. The six-year Egyptian campaign resulted in most League forces perishing.

Removal of League Treasury from Delos to Athens — 454 BC

In 454 BC, citing possible Phoenician Aegean raids, Athens moved the Delian League treasury from Delos to Athens — a significant development toward Empire.

Previously, the synod (council) controlled League funds. Now these became part of the Athenian treasury. Without consulting allies, Athenians:

  • Assessed tribute amounts from each state
  • Supervised annual income
  • Determined fund usage (e.g., one-sixtieth went to Athena’s sacred treasury)

Pericles additionally proposed placing 5,000 talents into a special Athenian building fund.

Opposition arose from Thucydides (not the historian), son of Melesias. He and supporters believed:

“Athens had lost her good name and disgraced herself by transferring from Delos into her own keeping the funds that had been contributed by the rest of Greece… The Greeks must be outraged… they must consider this an act of barefaced tyranny, when they see that with their own contributions, exhorted from them by force for the war against the Persians, are gilding and beautifying our city, as if it were some vain woman decking herself out with costly stones and statues and temples worth millions of money.”

— Plutarch, Pericles 12

Pericles’ Response

According to Plutarch, Pericles contended:

“Athens was not obliged to give the allies any account of how their money was spent, provided that they carried on the war for them and kept the Persians away. ‘They do not give us a single horse, nor a soldier, nor a ship. All they supply is money,’ he told the Athenians, ‘and this belongs not to the people who give it, but to those who receive it, so long as they provide the services they are paid for. It is no more than fair that after Athens has been equipped with all she needs to carry on the war, she should apply the surplus to public works, which, once completed, will bring her glory for all time, and while they are being built will convert that surplus to immediate use. In this way all kinds of enterprises and demands will be created which will provide inspiration for every art, find employment for every hand, and transform the whole people into wage-earners, so that the city will decorate and maintain herself at the same time from her own resources.’”

— Plutarch, Pericles 12

From this point, the allied council ceased meeting and Athens stopped consulting allies.

Return of Cimon and Five Year Truce between Athens and Sparta — 451 BC

Cimon returned from his 461 BC ostracism in 451 BC. His return and some allies’ revolts signalled policy changes. Since Cimon promoted Sparta peace, breathing space from Peloponnesian conflicts benefited Athens. A Five Year Truce with Sparta resulted.

However, lasting Greek peace required Athens relinquishing recently-gained Corinthian Gulf control. Pericles, committed to expanding Athenian power, refused. Corinth remained fearful and hostile.

Persia Defeated at Cyprus and Peace Negotiated with Persian Empire — 450–448 BC

Cimon resumed League forces leadership against Persia. A 200-ship fleet sailed to free Persian-controlled Cyprus.

Greeks besieged Kition, but Cimon died during the blockade (reportedly from disease). The fleet moved to Salamis (Cyprus) where it inflicted a double land and sea defeat on Phoenicians and Cilicians (Persian Empire members).

Cimon’s death marked a new period when “hostilities between Greek and Persian slumber.” Pericles-led Athenians now faced decisions regarding:

  • Maintaining ally control and preventing further revolts
  • Retaining recently-won Greek possessions and expanding influence
  • Preventing multi-front warfare challenges

They chose making peace with Persia over maintaining permanent Sparta peace. Maintaining permanent Peloponnesian peace seemed unlikely while Corinth resented Athenian Corinthian Gulf control.

An Athenian embassy (allegedly led by Callias, Cimon’s brother-in-law) went to Persia’s capital. The Peace of Callias (449–448 BC) stipulated: Persians agreed not to sail the Aegean Sea and Propontis, and to stay at least three days’ march from Asia Minor’s coast. Greeks were forbidden from attacking Persian territory.

With Persia no longer threatening, League members felt tribute contributions unnecessary. Athens disagreed, creating League crisis.

The Thirty Years’ Peace Treaty — 446–445 BC

Athenians needed more energy maintaining their naval empire, requiring lasting Peloponnesian peace. The Five Years’ Truce was expiring.

Pericles realised concessions were necessary. The resulting Thirty Years Peace treaty provided relative stability (though it lasted under 15 years before the 431 BC Peloponnesian War). The 440 BC Samos revolt nearly ended it much sooner.

Revolt of Samos — 440 BC

Sparta and Athens feared and envied each other. Sparta lacked Athens’ funds and sea power from its naval League, appearing willing to maintain peace until gaining military advantage.

Athens failed developing a Sparta-like land empire, focusing on League maintenance. Both lived in war readiness but had other priorities. The land-power/sea-power balance seemed to permit peaceful coexistence.

Samos, a large Delian League member island off Asia Minor’s coast, was a major ship supplier with oligarchic government.

A disagreement with neighbouring Miletus over Priene’s control escalated. Thucydides states Athens “sailed to Samos with forty ships” supporting Miletus’ claim.

Athens imposed drastic measures:

  • Changed Samos’ constitution from oligarchic to democratic
  • Took fifty boys and fifty men as hostages, placing them on Lemnos
  • Installed a garrison

Athenians then departed. However, fleeing oligarchs sought Persian Sardis governor support and raised 700 mercenaries. They recaptured Samos, “rescued the hostages from Lemnos and declared themselves independent,” preparing to attack Miletus. This stirred northern trouble — Byzantium revolted.

Before revolt spread further, Athens mobilised sixty ships led by Pericles and nine commanders. The fleet split: sixteen ships watched the Phoenician fleet in Caria while others got reinforcements from Chios and Lesbos. The main fleet defeated the seventy-ship Samian fleet returning from Miletus.

The revolt continued. Following a nine-month campaign with reinforcements and land–sea blockade, Athenians defeated the islanders.

Athens subsequently:

  • Confiscated remaining Samian ships
  • Demolished Samos’ fortifications
  • Forced hostage surrender and indemnity payment
  • Required Byzantium to resume subject status
