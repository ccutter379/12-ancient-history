Spartan Responses to Athenian Imperialism

Relations between Athens and Sparta from 465–440 BC

Athens–Sparta Conflicts: 465–440 BC

465 BC

Thasos revolts and Athens uses Delian League forces to lay siege; Sparta offers support to Thasos (the Spartans don't send troops)

464 BC

Earthquake and helot revolt in Sparta; Sparta asks for Athens' help but then sends the Athenian troops away when they arrive

461 BC

The pro-Spartan, Cimon, is ostracised from Athens

460 BC

  • Athens forms an alliance with Argos (traditional enemy of Sparta)
  • Megara joins the Delian League

459 BC

Helot rebels settled by Athens at Naupactus

457–6 BC

Athens conquers Aegina (a member of the Peloponnesian League)

456–5 BC

Troezen and Achaea (members of the Peloponnesian League) are brought under Athenian control

454 BC

Athens sends a military expedition to the Corinthian Gulf

451 BC

The Five Year Truce treaty is signed by Athens and Sparta

448–7 BC

Sacred War — Sparta takes temple of Apollo at Delphi from the Phocians and gives it to the Delphians; Athens returned the temple to the Phocians soon after

447 BC

Conflict between Athens and Sparta in Battles of Tanagra and Oenophyta

446–5 BC

  • The Five Year Truce expires; the Thirty Years Peace treaty is signed by Athens and Sparta
  • Megara revolts from Athens
  • Spartan forces enter Attica

440 BC

Samos revolts from Athens; the Peloponnesian League decide not to interfere

Following the Battle of Eurymedon, Athens leveraged Delian League forces beyond their original purpose of Persian defence. Under Pericles’ leadership from 461 BC onward, Athens pursued imperial expansion that fundamentally damaged Athens–Sparta relations.

Activity Questions

  1. Which event triggered the multiple conflicts between Peloponnesian and Delian League city-states?
  2. How concerned would Sparta have felt about the alliance between Athens and Argos? Why?

Threatening Athenian Actions

Sparta felt threatened by several Athenian behaviours:

  • Thasos seeking Spartan assistance (though troops never arrived)
  • Athens’ alliance with Argos, a powerful Peloponnesian state
  • Megara joining the Delian League
  • Settlement of helot rebels at Naupactus

Spartan Counter-Actions

Sparta also escalated tensions through:

  • Humiliating Athenian forces during helot revolts
  • Deploying troops to Boeotia in 457 BC, resulting in the Battles of Tanagra and Oenophyta

Historical Context

The conflicts between 465–446 BC were sometimes called the “First Peloponnesian War,” though historians now view these as “a series of smaller conflicts as opposed to a sustained war.”

The Five Years Truce (451 BC)

Cimon, recalled from exile, negotiated peace between Athens and Sparta. The treaty benefited both powers: Athens gained time to manage rebellious League members (increasing tribute-paying states), while Sparta strengthened Peloponnesian League ties and managed domestic helot unrest.

The Thirty Years Peace (446–445 BC)

After Athenian setbacks in Boeotia (447–446 BC), both powers recognised military realities: Athens dominated at sea; Sparta controlled land warfare. A thirty-year peace treaty formalised this understanding, preventing larger-scale conflict.
