Democratic Developments

Overview

Athens experienced significant democratic reforms alongside its imperial expansion, primarily from 465 BC onward under leaders like Ephialtes and Pericles. Key changes included ostracisms, restructured governing bodies, expanded political participation, and financial incentives for public service.

Major Reforms

Ostracism

Ostracism continued to be used in the 5th century BC as a method of protecting democracy, while also exiling political opponents. Notable figures like Themistocles, despite supporting democratic reforms, faced exile in 471 BC.

Weakening the Areopagus (462–461 BC)

Powers transferred from the aristocratic council to the Boule (500-member council) and Heliaea (people’s court), reducing the Areopagus to ceremonial functions. Archon positions, previously limited to wealthy classes, became more accessible through lottery selection by 458 BC.

Expanding Thetes’ Influence

The lowest citizen class gained wealth through naval service as rowers on triremes. Reforms made them eligible for high office, significantly increasing their political power and status.

Public Service Payment

From 461–460 BC, jurors received two obols daily, enabling poorer citizens to participate. Payment later extended to archons and council members, removing wealth restrictions from these positions.

Citizenship Restrictions (451 BC)

Pericles mandated both parents be Athenian citizens, legitimately married, with two years military cadet service required — actually restricting citizenship despite democratic expansion.

Economic Development

Naval expansion and building programmes created employment. Athens’ port of Piraeus, redesigned by Hippodamos using grid planning, became a Mediterranean trade hub with upgraded facilities and the Emporion marketplace.

Acropolis Reconstruction

After Xerxes destroyed temples during the Persian invasion, Pericles led the Acropolis rebuilding, notably replacing the Temple of Athena — a defining legacy.
