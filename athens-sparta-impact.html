Impact of the Persian Wars

Overview

Following Persia’s failed invasion of Greece, Athens and Sparta maintained relatively cordial relations, developing mutual respect through leaders like Leonidas and Themistocles. Sparta acknowledged Athens’ emerging naval dominance while recognising their own limitations at sea.

Sparta’s Response to the Delian League Formation

As Athens converted the Delian League into an imperial structure, Peloponnesian–Athenian relations deteriorated. According to Thucydides, Athens took action “against the Persians, some against their own allies when they revolted, some against the Peloponnesian Powers.”

The League defeated Persia decisively at Eurymedon (468 BC) and suppressed ally rebellions like Naxos’ attempted secession.

Spartan Acceptance of Athenian Naval Leadership

Sparta recognised its unsuitability for leading maritime operations because it:

  • Functioned primarily as a land-based military state
  • Faced serious internal difficulties
  • Had diminished influence among Peloponnesian League allies

Internal Instability in Sparta

The Persian Wars destabilised Sparta domestically. Leadership scandals included:

  • King Cleomenes allegedly participating in revolutionary activity
  • King Demaratus living in Persian exile, accompanying Xerxes to Greece
  • Regent Pausanias executed for treason
  • General Leotychidas banished for corruption after being caught accepting bribes

Sparta’s Lost Prestige

Sparta lost control of central and northern Peloponnesian allies, threatening its helot management system. Tegea aligned with traditional enemy Argos in some conflicts, while Elis and Mantinea adopted democratic governance — systems Sparta opposed due to fears of helot liberation movements.

Themistocles’ Anti-Spartan Stance (479–472 BC)

Wall Reconstruction

Themistocles deceived Sparta by secretly rebuilding Athenian fortifications while falsely assuring them construction wasn’t proceeding. The Spartans, though publicly composed, likely harboured resentment over Athens’ strengthening position.

Amphictyonic Council Opposition

After the wars, Sparta proposed expelling neutral or Persian-sympathising Greek states from the religious council. Themistocles blocked this motion, recognising Peloponnesian League dominance would result, offending Sparta and prompting their support for his rival Cimon.

Exile and Anti-Spartan Activities

Following his 472 BC ostracism, Themistocles moved to Argos and disseminated anti-Spartan messaging throughout the Peloponnese, possibly plotting against Sparta’s constitutional structure.

Cimon’s Pro-Spartan Policy (471–449 BC)

After Themistocles’ removal, Sparta supported Cimon through his:

  • Promotion of dual Greek leadership (Spartan military, Athenian naval)
  • Conservative political philosophy

Cimon, characterised as more Peloponnesian than Athenian, received Spartan honours and favours.

Assistance During Helot Revolt

Cimon led Athenian forces helping Sparta suppress the 465–464 BC helot uprising. Upon rejection by Sparta, he returned to Athens where his pro-Spartan reputation subsequently contributed to his ostracism.

Return and the Five Year Truce

When Cimon returned from exile in 451 BC with diminished influence, his Spartan connections enabled negotiation of the Five Year Truce — likely preventing direct conflict until the Peloponnesian War’s 431 BC outbreak.
