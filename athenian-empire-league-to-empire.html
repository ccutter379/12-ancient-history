From League to Empire

Introduction

Delian League from 477–468 BC

Reasons for formation

  • To continue the war with Persia and along the coasts of the Aegean and Asia Minor
  • Objectives were both offensive and defensive

League structure

  • Aristides to assess tribute
  • Cimon in charge of the League navy
  • Treasury and meetings at Delos
  • Member states swore oaths of allegiance

Activities against Persia

  • Byzantium
  • Eion
  • Caria and Lycia
  • Eurymedon

Activities against Greeks

  • Scyros
  • Carystus coerced into membership
  • Revolt and subjugation of Naxos

Between 477–468 BC, Athens led the Delian League with member states contributing either ships or money. After Naxos’s revolt, a third category emerged: subject states forced to pay tribute. By 450 BC, this alliance had transformed into an Athenian Empire, with resources redirected from Persian defence to Athenian interests. The Thasos revolt in 465 BC marked a pivotal transformation in League dynamics.

The Revolt of Thasos

Overview

Thasos was a prosperous northern Aegean island and major ship contributor to the League, wealthy in silver, vines, timber, and extensive mainland interests in Thrace with 7th-century colonies.

Secession Reasons

According to Thucydides, Thasos objected to Athenian claims on Strymon River resources and trade. The conflict escalated when Athens attempted to establish a 10,000-citizen colony called “The Nine Ways,” threatening Thasos’s economy and regional influence.

Siege and External Support

Cimon led League forces in a 2–3 year siege. Macedonia offered assistance, while Sparta secretly promised armed intervention in Attica — though an earthquake and helot revolt prevented Spartan action.

Surrender Terms (463 BC)

Thasos was forced to:

  • Demolish walls
  • Surrender ships
  • Close its mint
  • Pay indemnity
  • Relinquish independence
  • Pay future tribute
  • Surrender mainland trading rights

Historical Significance

Four key consequences:

  1. Athens demonstrated members couldn’t leave the League
  2. League forces were used against member states
  3. Sparta–Athens tensions escalated
  4. Internal Athenian politics shifted toward radical democracy under Ephialtes, challenging Cimon’s conservative approach

Political Context

Post-siege, radical democrats accused Cimon of accepting Macedonian bribes to avoid annexation. Though acquitted, Athens’ movement toward fuller democracy made his continued influence unlikely.
